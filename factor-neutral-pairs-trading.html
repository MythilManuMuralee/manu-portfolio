Technical Deep Dive

Factor-Neutral Pairs Trading Pipeline

A production-grade algorithmic pipeline uniting Unsupervised Machine Learning and Time-Series Econometrics.

SECTION 1 — PROJECT MOTIVATION

Pairs trading is an equity-neutral statistical arbitrage strategy. By identifying two highly correlated assets, going long on the underperformer and short on the overperformer, one can theoretically extract profit regardless of broader market direction (beta-neutrality). However, naive pairs trading—relying merely on price correlation—carries a catastrophic flaw: the risk of permanent structural divergence. If two companies drift apart because of fundamental underlying shifts (e.g., changes in debt ratios, shifts in core business models), they will never revert to a historical mean, and the trade will bleed capital.

Combining fundamental machine learning screening with econometric cointegration testing solves this mathematically. Before looking at prices, we use unsupervised clustering to prove two companies are "fundamental twins." Only then do we conduct rigorous time-series tests to algebraically prove mean-reverting behavior.

SECTION 2 — PHASE 1: DATA ENGINEERING

The pipeline dynamically scrapes the S&P 500 constituency from live HTML sources. Utilizing the Python requests library, the pipeline simulates a standard web browser via a User-Agent header. A raw pandas read_html() Wikipedia scrape typically returns a 403 Forbidden error because modern cloud-delivery endpoints aggressively filter non-browser automated requests. Bypassing this filter ensures robust programmatic execution.

Financial ratios—Beta, Market Capitalization, Trailing P/E, and Debt/Equity—are asynchronously sourced via the yfinance API. Because financial reporting is notoriously noisy, missing data is unavoidable. The pipeline applies SimpleImputer(strategy='median'). Median imputation is vastly superior to mean imputation in financial datasets, as trailing outliers (such as a 10,000x P/E ratio due to virtually zero earnings) fatally distort the mean, whereas the median remains topologically resilient.

SECTION 3 — PHASE 2: UNSUPERVISED ML CLUSTERING

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) was specifically selected over K-Means. K-Means forcibly assigns every single data point to a cluster. In the context of equity fundamentals, forcing outliers into pairs guarantees catastrophic correlation failure.

DBSCAN seamlessly identifies dense spatial structures and discards everything else as noise (assigned the label -1). By deploying hyperparameters eps=1.5 and min_samples=2, the model geometrically strictly constrains the maximum distance (epsilon) between fundamental feature vectors required to form a cluster. Consequently, we ensure we are only matching companies that are virtually identical mathematically inside an n-dimensional fundamental space.

SECTION 4 — PHASE 3: ENGLE-GRANGER COINTEGRATION

In plain English, cointegration means that while two assets may wander randomly over time, the distance (spread) between them is tethered to a stable equilibrium; if they drift apart, they will predictably snap back together. Correlation simply measures vector alignment; correlation is not cointegration. Two strictly correlated assets might diverge indefinitely at an angle, failing to ever mean-revert.

The pipeline employs the two-step Engle-Granger methodology:

Step 1: OLS Regression. An Ordinary Least Squares regression (Y = βX + α) models the price of stock Y against stock X. The beta coefficient (slope) represents the dynamic Hedge Ratio—exactly how many shares of X to short for every share of Y bought, neutralizing market exposure.

Step 2: Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test. The residuals (the pricing error from the OLS regression) are extracted and subjected to the ADF test. Testing the residuals isolates the pure 'spread.' If this spread is proven to be stationary (no unit root), it mathematically proves mean-reversion.

We established a strong p-value threshold of 0.10. In this context, a 90% statistical confidence asserts that there is less than a 10% chance the observed mean-reversion of the spread is happening purely by random walk noise.
